East Side Neighborhood's Big Glow Up Signals Bright Future


After decades of disinvestment and economic stagnation, Islandview, the East Side  neighborhood gem, is finally getting the attention many say it has long deserved. With a slate of new developments underway and a wave of newcomers arriving from Chicago, Ferndale, and “the creative class diaspora,” local leaders are calling it a turning point for the neighborhood.


“Islandview is no longer a secret,” said Erin Langston, Director of Urban Partnerships for Forward Detroit, a nonprofit affiliated with seven real estate investment firms. “It’s a canvas of opportunity, and we’re excited to see what happens when innovation meets, er, legacy.”


The neighborhood, located just west of West Village and north of Belle Isle, is now home to several new “mixed-use enclaves”—replacing what used to be empty lots, auto shops, and “excessively personal” homes, where families have had the audacity to live for generations. 


"We Always Knew This Place Had Potential"


Longtime residents learned about the changes coming to their historic neighborhood through a formal relocation order distributed Wednesday morning. The notice used colorful vocabulary to describe their communities as “up-and-coming,” “formerly gritty,” and “an undiscovered gem just waiting for the right demographic.”


“This area has so much soul,” said Becca Sorensen, a freelance UX healer and recent transplant from Kalamazoo. “You can just feel the authenticity when you step outside and see, like, actual struggle. It's been so inspiring for my vision board.”


"We saw so much raw character here,” says Shayne, the 29-year-old Creative Director of a Alpaca skin shoe-and-rescue company, and recent arrival to the area. “There was this tent encampment being cleared from the park, and a taco truck  that stops just down the street, and I just thought: this is where I want to raise my emotional support corgi.”


His partner, Harper (pronouns: gentry/gentryself), agrees. “We just felt called to give back. So we opened an artisanal dog treat apothecary in what used to be the rec hall. It’s called Woof Justice - because every pup deserves ethical snacks."


New Business Boom: Gritwashing for the Soul


Already, signs of economic rebirth abound. The laundromat is now a salt cave offering "urban cleansing experiences". The chicken shack is now a chicken concept restaurant where single wings cost $14. And the corner liquor store has partnered with a wellness startup to sell $18 bottles of "Detroit-Style Water" -- which is just tap water in mason jars, with inspirational quotes about resilience.


The neighborhood barbershop, once a cornerstone of community life, has been rebranded as a "heritage grooming speakeasy." Cuts start at $70 and include a complimentary oat milk cortado and unsolicited podcast recommendations about entrepreneurial mindfulness. 


Affordable Housing? Already Handled.


With the opening of The Bricks at Bellevue, a 46-unit “heritage-inspired loft community,” and Café Grime, a local espresso bar operating out of a repurposed bus terminal, Islandview is attracting attention for its “urban grit" aesthetic and surprisingly affordable monthly rent - starting at just $2,800 for a studio spaces.


Developers insist that longtime residents are not being pushed out, but gently redirected toward “alternative housing solutions,” including relocation grants worth approximately three months of groceries and digital vouchers for future residency in neighborhoods “with similar character-building challenges.”


“We’re very conscious of maintaining the culture here and we absolutely do not just push people out of their homes,” said Mark Lennox, project manager for Revive East, LLC. “That’s why a full 8% of all new units are designated as affordable -- for households making only $65,000 a year."


Correction: An earlier version of this article stated that no residents were displaced. Infact, 62 homes were "de-prioritized for occupancy" to make room for a pet-friendly sculpture park featuring abstract art.


Community Resources Get a Makeover


For those who can’t relocate, public resources are being reimagined eith the modern resident in mind. The local rec center will soon host "Gratitude and Gentrification" seminars and has replaced its GED program with glassblowing workshops. 


Longtime businesses have either been shuttered or successfully “re-imagined.” The corner store at Vernor and Field is now a zero-waste vermouth bar with a two-drink minimum. A former halfway house is being converted into a dual-purpose co-living and candle-making space called The Commune @ Charlevoix.


“We’re not erasing history,” said Forward Detroit’s Arie Langston. “We’re rewriting it in cursive script on exposed brick.”


Residents Share Mixed Reactions


Some longtime residents say they feel left out of the changes -- a sentiment city officials describe as "transitory anxiety"


“I’ve lived here thirty years. I used to know everyone on this block,” said Deborah McCaskill, a retired postal worker whose home is now flanked by two short-term rentals and a boutique that sells $42 socks. “Now I can’t afford to shop at the grocery store they built for us.”


When asked about community input, city officials pointed to last summer’s Gentrification & You! listening tour, which included a Q&A and complimentary hummus bowls at a members-only rooftop.


A Vision for the Future


City Councilmember Zachary Bell, who represents the district, praised the transformation at a recent community meeting help at 2PM on a Tuesday. 


“We see Islandview as a model of inclusive prosperity,” Bell said during a ribbon-cutting for an all-electric silent streetcar that will run exactly two blocks. “This is what happens when private enterprise, philanthropic capital, and culture-forward branding align with community needs."


A Message from the Mayor


“This isn’t gentrification,” said Mayor Mike Duggan while breaking ground on the ninth micro-loft development called “The Silos," each unit a cozy 320 square feet of "intentional living space." “This is opportunity. This is prosperity. This is us finally catching up to the property values of Portland.”


He closed the ceremony by tossing a commemorative eviction notice into the air.


The next phase of development includes a wellness trail paved with locally sourced quartz, a tech incubator inside a former elementary school, and an “immersive Afro-futurist speakeasy” designed in collaboration with three interns from Grand Valley State University.


The Bottom Line


So get excited, neighbors. Transformation isn’t just coming to your block—it’s coming for your block.
And when you’re priced out, take comfort in knowing: you helped make it cool enough for someone else. Your authenticity has been successfully harvested. Your job is finished. You can rest now. We'll take it from here.
